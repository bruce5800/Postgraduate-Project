AN INTERACTIVE GUIDE · THESIS CHAPTER 9 / APPENDIX B

Where you can test the advice,
you don't need it

Some online-matching algorithms are handed a prediction about the future, watch a short sample to check whether it looks trustworthy, and then commit. The thesis proves a hard limit on this idea. This page breaks the proof into five gadgets you can play with — drag the sliders and you will run into the wall yourself. No background needed beyond “what's an average.”

THE WHOLE ARGUMENT IN THREE LINES
  1. Test-and-fallback algorithms peek at a small prefix of the input, then either trust the prediction for everything that remains, or ignore it entirely.
  2. The theorem: if the peek is small (sublinear), no decision rule whatsoever can both cash in when the prediction is good and stay safe when it's bad.
  3. The reason: inputs simple enough to test a prediction on are exactly the inputs where you'd do nearly perfectly without one. Testability and “nothing to gain” are the same property.

The game · what the algorithm is allowed to do

Watch k arrivals, then bet the night

Picture a taxi rank on New Year's Eve. Passengers arrive one by one, and each must be put into a suitable car immediately — no take-backs. Before the night starts, a tipster sells you a forecast of tonight's crowd. You're allowed to watch the first k passengers for free. Then you must choose, once and for all: run the whole night by the tip sheet, or throw it away and dispatch the way you always have.

That's the entire setup, formally: n requests arrive; the algorithm sees the first k (with k much smaller than n), applies any rule it likes to what it saw, and commits to one of two modes:

Follow: route every remaining request the way the prediction suggests or  Fall back: ignore the prediction, use the standard no-advice method (Ranking)

A good algorithm should be consistent — when the tip is accurate, it collects nearly all the profit the tip makes possible — and robust — when the tip is garbage, it loses nothing compared to never having had a tip. Chapter 6 of the thesis found experimentally that no tuning achieves both. This page explains why that's not a tuning problem. It's mathematically impossible.

Building block · Appendix B.3 / Lemma B.2

One tiny dilemma — the only moment advice ever matters

Two parking bays, a and b. A hatchback that fits either bay arrives first and must park now. Later, maybe a van shows up (fits only bay a), or a trailer (only bay b) — or nobody comes. If you parked the hatchback in the wrong bay, the special vehicle drives away unserved. The tip sheet tells you which one to expect.

The whole hard instance in the proof is just thousands of these dilemmas glued together. In each cell: the flexible request F commits first; then with probability θs comes specialist α (fits only a), with probability θ(1−s) specialist β (only b), otherwise nobody. The advice tells you the likely direction — which side to keep free.

Adding it up · Appendix B.4 / Lemma B.3

An exact exchange rate: advice error → lost profit

Glue thousands of parking dilemmas into one long night. The tip sheet gets a fraction φ of them pointed the wrong way. How do the night's takings depend on φ? Not roughly — exactly, on a straight line.
E[profit when following] = ρperfect − ½·ℓ1(p,q) In words: start from the perfect-advice score, subtract exactly half the advice's total error. Verified numerically to three decimals (parameters θ=0.6, ε=0.3 from the thesis).

The barrier · Chapter 9.3, pillar 3 / Theorem B.5 (citing Canonne et al., COLT 2022)

The sampling wall: testability and “nothing to gain” are one knob

To trust the tipster you must judge, from k = 200 free sightings, whether their forecast of the whole night is roughly right. If tonight's crowd has only 8 kinds of passenger, 200 sightings is plenty — about 25 looks at each kind. If it has 4,000 kinds, most kinds you will never see even once.

Statistics makes this precise, and brutal. Checking a forecast is exactly right costs about √r samples (cheap). But checking it's roughly right — “close enough to help vs. far enough to hurt,” which is what the green ball forces on us — costs about r/log r samples: nearly as many as learning the whole crowd from scratch. And here's the twist that makes the theorem:

Crowds simple enough to test are crowds where you didn't need the tip — a few common passenger types are easy to dispatch near-perfectly by habit. Crowds where the tip could genuinely help have too many rare types to test.

The clincher · Appendix B.2 / Lemma B.1 (fully proved)

Two tipsters you can't tell apart

Final move. Build two possible worlds: in world G the tipster is honest (following wins you δ), in world Bd the tipster is subtly poisoned (following costs you Δ). The sampling wall guarantees we can build them so their first-k arrivals look almost identical — any statistic you compute from your peek differs between the worlds by at most a sliver, γk. Now: you still have to pick Follow or Fall back.
(1−ηc) ≤ ηr + γk In words: (upside you give up) + (damage you let through) ≥ 1 − (how much the worlds differ on your peek). When γk ≈ 0, the two losses must sum to ≈ everything.

This holds for any rule computed from the peek — a threshold, a neural net, anything — which is what makes the theorem algorithm-independent.
Assembly · Chapter 9.4 / Theorem B.6

Bolting the four parts together

① The construction (Gadgets A + B; proved, numerically verified) — there's a family of instances where advice with error ≤ a genuinely helps and error ≥ b genuinely hurts, with a real gap between, and Θ(n) distinct request types.
↓ the reduction (Lemma B.4): your peek is literally an i.i.d. sample, so your decision is a statistical test
② Any algorithm that's both consistent and robust would be a tolerant tester — from k samples it would distinguish “roughly right” from “badly wrong.”
↓ the sampling wall (Gadget C; exact modern result, COLT 2022)
③ That test needs ~n/log n samples — more than any sublinear peek provides. So two witness worlds exist that require opposite decisions yet look the same (γk→0).
↓ the trade-off inequality (Gadget D; fully proved)
④ The two losses must sum to ≈ 1. No test-and-fallback algorithm — under any decision rule — is both consistent and robust. The achievable region collapses to “just play the baseline.”

Upside exists only where the test is impossible. Where you can test the advice, you don't need it.

HONEST STATUS (mirrors thesis §9.5 / §B.7)
The trade-off inequality, the single-cell constants, and the exact exchange-rate law are proved (the latter two also verified numerically to three decimals). The tolerant-testing lower bound is a cited, exact, modern result (Canonne–Jain–Kamath–Li, COLT 2022). One routine verification step remains — writing out the two witness distributions the lower bound guarantees and checking they land at errors a and b — plus a final end-to-end review. A low-dimensional variant of the construction provably fails (a single shared bias is too easy to estimate), so the many-types regime is essential; a constant-r version is an open problem.
